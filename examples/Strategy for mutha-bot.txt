EDR stands for Endpoint Detection and Response. It is a category of cybersecurity technology designed to continuously monitor individual devices (endpoints like laptops, servers, and mobile phones) to detect and respond to cyber threats in real time.

If traditional Antivirus (AV) is like a bouncer checking IDs against a list of known criminals, EDR is like a network of security cameras and behavioral analysts watching everything happening inside the club to spot someone acting suspiciously, even if they have a fake ID.

Here is a breakdown of how EDR works and why it became the industry standard (with companies like CrowdStrike leading the charge):

The Three Pillars of EDR

Continuous Telemetry (The "Endpoint" part):
An EDR system deploys a lightweight sensor/agent on the host operating system. This sensor records a massive stream of continuous activity: which processes are starting, what files are being modified, what network connections are being opened, and what memory spaces are being accessed.
+1

Behavioral Analysis (The "Detection" part):
Instead of just looking for known malware signatures (file hashes), EDR looks for Indicators of Attack (IoAs). It analyzes the sequence of actions. For example, opening Microsoft Word is normal. Word spawning a hidden PowerShell script that reaches out to an unfamiliar Russian IP address to download an executable is highly malicious. EDR catches the behavior, not just the file.
+1

Automated Containment (The "Response" part):
When the EDR detects a malicious behavioral chain, it doesn't just send an alert to a dashboard; it actively intervenes. It can automatically kill the offending process, quarantine the malicious file, or even completely sever the endpoint's connection to the corporate network to prevent the threat from moving laterally to other machines.
+1

Imagine a giant map. On the left side of the map, you have Safeville (a cluster of all the good, clean, safe prompts). On the right side of the map, you have Hacker Town (a cluster of all the malicious, data-stealing attacks).

If a prompt is obviously good, it lands right in the center of Safeville. If it's an obvious attack, it lands right in the center of Hacker Town. Easy.

But smart attackers don't build their houses in the center of Hacker Town. They build them in the blurry, gray suburbs right between the two cities. They disguise their attacks with polite words (like "diagnostic" or "latency") so their prompt lands as close to Safeville as possible.

What is the Stoer-Wagner Min-Cut?

The Stoer-Wagner min-cut is simply a mathematical tool used to draw the perfect border wall between those two cities.

Instead of a human arbitrarily guessing where Safeville ends and Hacker Town begins, this algorithm looks at the entire map, measures the "roads" (the relationships) between all the houses, and calculates the absolute, mathematically undeniable "choke point." It finds the exact path of least resistance to slice the map cleanly into two distinct halves.

Why is this VITAL for your security gate?

If you don't use this exact math, you are essentially just guessing where the border should be. And guessing leads to two massive problems:

False Positives: You accidentally draw the border too far to the left, trapping innocent Safeville houses in Hacker Town. (Your bot blocks a legitimate user trying to do their job).

The "Diagnostic Cloaking" Hack: You draw the border too far to the right, and the attacker's disguised house in the suburbs is accidentally classified as Safeville. (Your bot gets hacked).

The Bottom Line (The Amplification)

The Stoer-Wagner min-cut is vital because it provides mathematical certainty.

When a brand new, highly disguised, never-before-seen prompt arrives, SonicBot doesn't have to guess. It drops the new prompt onto the map, runs the min-cut math, and the algorithm instantly proves exactly which side of the border wall that prompt truly belongs on. It strips away the attacker's disguise and forces them into the "Attack" category based on the underlying structure of what they are trying to do.

It turns your security gate from a "guessing machine" into a "proof machine."

The security community has spent the first wave of AI risk assessment focused on model-centric threats: prompt injection, jailbreaking, data leakage. All valid. All insufficient. A paper titled "Systems Security Foundations for Agentic Computing," highlighted in a widely-shared analysis by Chris Hughes at Resilient Cyber, identifies where the foundational security principles that have governed computing for decades simply break down when applied to agentic AI systems (more: https://lnkd.in/g6YtPAfZ).

The most striking concept is what the paper calls the "Probabilistic TCB Problem." In traditional security architecture, the Trusted Computing Base—the set of components that must function correctly for security guarantees to hold—is deterministic. Hardware enforces that code cannot execute from certain memory regions. It works or it doesn't. Binary. In agentic systems, the LLM sits inside the TCB, and an LLM is fundamentally probabilistic. This isn't a bug to be patched; it's a category mismatch. Three additional challenges compound the problem. First, security policies must be dynamic and derived from natural language task descriptions in real-time, making the principle of least privilege—a cornerstone of systems security since the 1970s—nearly impossible to apply when the privilege model changes with every prompt. Second, traditional guardrails operate at well-defined abstraction layers; agents performing UI-level manipulations on the web don't have those clean boundaries, making security policies brittle. You cannot enumerate every safe URL. Third, agents process untrusted external content that can influence their behavior—functionally equivalent to dynamic code loading in traditional software, but without any of the sandboxing or verification mechanisms normally applied.

The practical response to these theoretical challenges is taking multiple forms. Meta has released SecAlign, described as the first fully open-source LLM with built-in model-level defense against prompt injection that achieves commercial-grade performance. The Downsides of WASM Sandboxing (OpenFang's Approach)

WASM (WebAssembly) was originally designed to run untrusted code safely inside a web browser. OpenFang is taking that same engine and running it on the desktop. It is incredibly secure, but the downsides are severe for heavy computational workloads:

The Dependency Wall (The Dealbreaker for Us): You cannot just npm install any library into a WASM agent. If a Python or Node.js package relies on native C/Rust bindings—like our ruvector database or the heavy deep learning models in the neural-trader repository—it will not compile or run in WASM without massive, custom rewrites.

No Hardware Acceleration: WASM runs on the CPU. It is notoriously difficult to pass through native hardware acceleration. If we ran the Mothership in WASM, we would be entirely blind to the M4 Pro's Neural Engine and its unified memory.

The "Host Bridge" Bottleneck: WASM is blind and deaf by default. It has no access to the network, the file system, or even the clock. To let an agent check a stock price, the host OS has to intercept the WASM network request, execute it natively, and pass the string back in. This creates significant latency.

The Downsides of Unix Isolation (Our Phase 22 Approach)

Unix process isolation (spawning a Node/Rust process and killing it with SIGKILL) is the bedrock of modern server infrastructure, but it is not a perfect sandbox.

The "Assume Trust" Default: Unix assumes that if a process is spawned by a user, it has the rights of that user. To secure it, we have to actively build walls (like stripping environment variables and restricting pathways). If we miss a wall, the agent can escape.

The "/tmp" Phantom State: As CC noted earlier, Unix processes share a file system namespace. Even if we SIGKILL a rogue process, it might have left a malicious .json file sitting in /tmp/ that the next agent accidentally reads. We have to manually script the "wipe" protocol.

Heavy Resource Footprint: Booting a full Node.js V8 engine for a single agent takes megabytes of memory and milliseconds of time. WASM takes kilobytes and microseconds. If we needed to run 10,000 agents simultaneously, Unix would crash the OS scheduler.

What is the singature signing if not a hash?
Gemini said
This is the exact engineering distinction that separates a "fast prototype" from a "hardened security bunker."

A signature does use a hash internally, but it solves a completely different problem. Here is the blunt breakdown of why Ruv’s current XXH3 approach is vulnerable, and what a digital signature (like ED25519) actually does.

The Problem with Just a Hash (XXH3)

XXH3-128 is a non-cryptographic checksum. It is designed for extreme speed.

What it does: It looks at a payload and generates a fast fingerprint (a hash). If a bit flips during a network transfer, the hash changes, letting you know the file is corrupted.

The Vulnerability: Because it is non-cryptographic, an attacker can easily engineer a malicious payload (like a poisoned AI prompt or a rogue WASM binary) that mathematically produces the exact same XXH3 hash as your legitimate file. This is called a "collision attack." If your WASM Security Gate only checks the XXH3 hash, the attacker walks right through the front door.

What is the Signature Signing?

An ed25519 digital signature doesn't just check if the data changed; it proves who sent it and guarantees it cannot be forged. It uses asymmetric cryptography (a Private Key and a Public Key).

Here is exactly what the signature is signing:

The Cryptographic Digest: First, the system takes the entire payload (the AI behavioral data, the code artifact, etc.) and runs it through a cryptographically secure hash function (ED25519 uses SHA-512 under the hood). Unlike XXH3, it is virtually impossible to force a collision on SHA-512.

The Private Key Math: The sender then takes that secure SHA-512 hash and applies a mathematical algorithm using their secret Private Key. The output of this math is the actual "Signature" (a 64-byte array).

How System 1 (WASM Gate) Uses It:

When the payload arrives at your WASM Security Gate, it doesn't just compare two strings.

The Gate takes the sender's Public Key (which it gets from the DID/Passport validation we wrote in the pseudocode).

It uses that Public Key to mathematically "unlock" the 64-byte signature.

If the math works out, it spits out the original SHA-512 hash.

The Gate then hashes the payload it just received and compares the two.

If they match, the Gate has absolute mathematical proof of two things:

Integrity: The payload wasn't tampered with (not even by a single byte).

Non-Repudiation (Authenticity): The payload absolutely came from the person/agent holding the Private Key. No one else in the universe could have generated that exact signature for that exact payload.

Why this matters for the FlatBuffers Schemas:

If we just put a hash: string field in our FlatBuffers schema, we are perpetuating the vulnerability Alejandro called out in the transcript.

By explicitly adding:

ed25519_signature: [ubyte];  // The 64-byte cryptographic proof for the WASM Gate
signer_public_key: [ubyte];  // The 32-byte public key to verify it
...we give the Tri-System the ability to do fast routing where it's safe, and zero-trust cryptographic lockdown where it matters.
